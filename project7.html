Irradiation Regimes and Microbial Survival

Astrobiology project at the German Aerospace Center (DLR)

Overview

During my internship at the German Aerospace Center (DLR) in Cologne, I worked on a project that investigated the effects of different irradiation regimes on the survival and genomic integrity of astrobiologically significant microorganisms. The study was motivated by central questions in astrobiology: how microorganisms respond to the extreme radiation environments of space and what this means for the habitability of planetary surfaces and for planetary protection policies. The project was supervised by Dr. Stefan Leuko.

The project bridged microbiology, radiation biology, and space science by focusing on organisms with high relevance to survival in extraterrestrial conditions. Understanding their limits under simulated space irradiation is crucial to assessing the likelihood of life transfer between planets and designing sterilization protocols for spacecraft.

Figure 1. German Aerospace Center (DLR), Cologne – experimental setup for growth cureve measurements.

Methods

I participated in designing and conducting experiments using controlled irradiation systems at DLR. Microorganisms were exposed to different types of radiation, including:

  • UV radiation to simulate solar exposure in planetary environments.
  • Ionizing radiation such as gamma rays, mimicking cosmic radiation.
  • Combined irradiation regimes representing complex extraterrestrial scenarios.

Survival rates were assessed through colony-forming unit (CFU) counts and metabolic assays, while DNA integrity was tested using gel electrophoresis and molecular markers for damage and repair. These experiments provided a model for how space-like irradiation affects both the viability and genetic stability of microbes.

Key Results

  • Identified species-specific differences in microbial survival under UV and ionizing radiation.
  • Showed that some microorganisms retained partial genomic stability even under extreme irradiation stress.
  • Highlighted the role of DNA repair mechanisms as a critical factor in microbial persistence.
  • Contributed to ongoing DLR research on planetary protection and life-detection missions.

Impact

This project contributed to astrobiology research by clarifying how microbial life can survive or adapt under extreme radiation. The findings inform:

  • Planetary protection protocols – preventing forward contamination during space missions.
  • Astrobiological models – assessing potential for microbial survival on Mars or icy moons.
  • Biotechnology applications – understanding radiation resistance mechanisms for applied sciences.

The internship also gave me the opportunity to gain hands-on experience with advanced radiation facilities, collaborate with interdisciplinary teams at DLR, and contribute to real-world challenges at the intersection of microbiology and space exploration.
