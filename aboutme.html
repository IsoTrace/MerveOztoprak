About Me

Who I Am

Hi, I’m Merve Tomečková Öztoprak — a bioanalytical scientist with a passion for exploring life’s hidden chemical stories. I’m currently completing my PhD at NIOZ / Utrecht University, where I develop new mass spectrometry workflows (LC–Orbitrap MS) to study isotopic fingerprints of lipids. My work bridges analytical chemistry, data science, and environmental forensics, and I love pushing the boundaries of how we can use chemistry to answer questions about origins, processes, and impacts of organisms on our earth systems and each other.

Coming from a cross-disciplinary background that spans ecosystem sciences, forestry, geobiology, and evolutionary biology, I developed a fascination for using analytical chemistry to explore life’s hidden traces. During my studies at the Universities of Göttingen, Uppsala, and Munich , I became captivated by how isotope and molecular tools can reveal some of the oldest biosignatures on Earth — a perspective that continues to inspire my work in tracing invisible biosignatures today. This curiosity has guided me through research experiences at Caltech, the Australian National University, and BayerCropScience, in addition to internships and research project with partners in Svalbard, Hong Kong, and Italy. Along the way I became fluent in a wide range of techniques — from LC-MS and GC-MS to IRMS, applied omics, statistical modeling in R/Python, microbiology to ecology — and learned how to work across cultures, fields, and disciplines. I thrive in collaborative, international settings, and I’m motivated by turning innovative analytical ideas into tools that matter, whether in environmental science, health, or forensic applications.

Outside of the lab, I enjoy spending time hiking, painting, and traveling to spend time with family and friends across the globe. As a recent mom, spending time fostering my daughter’s curiosity and seeing the world through her eyes reminds me of the wonder and creativity that also drive my work as a scientist.

I strongly believe that as academics we have a responsibility not only to advance knowledge through our research, but also to use our training in critical thinking and our experience working across disciplines with diverse stakeholders to improve the communities around us. To this end, I have contributed to the institutions where I have worked by founding diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives, instructing colleagues and students through workshops, lectures, and supervision, and serving the wider scientific community by co-organizing conferences and community initiatives.

I am convinced that we can overcome today’s greatest challenges — from the climate crisis to rising political tensions and growing humanitarian needs — if we resist nihilism and continue to empower the next generation of academics, leaders, and citizens to rigorously apply scientific methods and fact-based approaches, ensuring that solutions and innovations are grounded in evidence and guided by a spirit of shared responsibility for respectful and just treatment of others.
