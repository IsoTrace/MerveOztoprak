Earthworm Diversity and Climate Impact

DAAD RISE Worldwide Project at CUHK, Hong Kong

Overview

As a DAAD RISE scholarship holder, I spent the summer of 2016 at the Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK), Department of Geography & Resource Management. My project focused on earthworm diversity and the effects of climate change on soil ecosystems. Earthworms are widely recognized as ecosystem engineers: they alter soil structure, accelerate organic matter turnover, and regulate greenhouse gas fluxes through their burrowing and casting activity. These functions are critical to global carbon and nitrogen cycles, influencing CO2 sequestration, nitrous oxide emissions, and long-term soil fertility. As climate change intensifies rainfall variability and temperature extremes, earthworm-mediated processes become increasingly important for predicting ecosystem feedbacks to the atmosphere.

At CUHK, I had the opportunity to collaborate with leading researchers including Prof. Daniel Lai and Dr. Michelle Law. Their research highlights how subtropical soil biota—especially Amynthas earthworms—respond to wetting–drying cycles, driving short-term pulses of carbon and nitrogen fluxes that scale up to influence regional greenhouse-gas budgets. Documenting local species composition and linking it to functional outcomes under climate-stress scenarios not only advances soil ecology but also supports policy-relevant predictions of ecosystem resilience in rapidly urbanizing subtropical regions.

Figure 1. Field and lab work on earthworm diversity across contrasting landscapes in Hong Kong at CUHK.

Methods

The project combined field surveys and lab-based climate simulations. We collected earthworm samples across different ecosystems and elevations in Hong Kong and performed species identification. In addition, I designed and maintained in vitro climate experiments using PVC soil columns with controlled moisture and temperature conditions. These experiments simulated predicted climate change scenarios, such as longer summers, heavier rainfall, and prolonged droughts.

Key Results

  • Documented differences between invasive European Lumbricus and native Amynthas species.
  • Showed that invasive Lumbricus species enrich only the topsoil, while Amynthas improve deeper aeration.
  • Built experimental systems that allowed reproducible climate stress tests on earthworm populations.
  • Gained first-hand experience with soil mesofauna and their role in carbon cycling.

Impact

This project provided insights into how climate change might alter soil biodiversity and ecosystem services in subtropical regions. The experience also gave me valuable exposure to interdisciplinary research in ecology, soil science, and climate studies. Beyond the scientific results, the scholarship offered me a unique opportunity to live and work in Hong Kong, engage with an international research team, and explore cultural exchange through shared passion for research.
