Site-Specific Isotope Analysis of Phytane using the GC–Orbitrap

Independent research project at Caltech

Overview

In collaboration with Caltech, I developed a novel GC–Orbitrap workflow that enabled the first reproducible position-specific isotope ratio analysis (PSIA) of the sedimentary biomarker phytane. This study demonstrated that Orbitrap high-resolution mass spectrometry can recover intramolecular δ13C patterns from hydrocarbon fragments at nanomolar concentrations.

A - Schematic illustration of the direct elution method, where the analyte is introduced to the ion source of the MS directly after elution from the GC column. B - schematic illustration of the “peak broadener” or “peak capture” method. C - MS2 chromatogram of phytol measured in “peak broadener” mode. AQS set to 50-300 m/z. D - AQS set to 80-90 m/z focusing on the C6 fragment of Phytol.

Methods

Using a modified GC–Orbitrap system with a trapping reservoir, we captured phytane during elution and extended its signal over 60 minutes, allowing for high-precision isotope analysis of the diagnostic C6H13+ fragment (m/z 85). Baseline separation from overlapping isotopologues ensured accurate quantification. The method was validated against GC–IRMS averages and processed with custom R pipelines for filtering, error propagation, and comparison with theoretical limits.

Key Results

  • Achieved high precision isotope ratios (SE <0.5‰, SD <1.5‰) using <15 nmol phytane.
  • Showed consistent differences between phytane from Anacystis (cyanobacteria), Halobacterium, and a commercial standard.
  • Demonstrated that fragment isotope ratios align with molecular average δ13C values, confirming biosynthetic origins.
  • Established Orbitrap PSIA as a viable tool for hydrocarbons beyond amino acids and small molecules.

Impact

This project represents a proof-of-concept for PSIA of sedimentary biomarkers. By targeting diagnostic fragments, it is possible to deconvolve the isotopic fingerprints of phytane’s biosynthetic precursors, distinguishing sources such as chlorophyll versus archaeal lipids.
