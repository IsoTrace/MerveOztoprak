Reconstructions of Precambrian Ecosystems using Isotopic & Biomarker signatures

Australian National University Project

Overview

During my MSc research at the Australian National University, I carried out field and laboratory investigations on Precambrian core fragments from Western Australia. The project aimed to reconstruct microbial ecosystems in Archaean and Proterozoic strata using a combined approach of biomarker geochemistry and compound-specific isotope analysis. The project was supervised by Prof. Jochen Brocks

Methods

Organic extracts were analyzed using gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS) with electron ionization (70 eV, m/z 50–800). Data acquisition was processed in Thermo Xcalibur. Screening targeted n-alkanes, hopanes, steranes, and aromatic steroids. Compound-specific isotope analysis (δ13C) was applied to selected biomarkers to investigate metabolic pathways. Maturity indicators such as sterane isomer ratios and hopane Ts/Tm were evaluated to detect alteration and contamination.

Key Results

  • Resolved biomarker assemblages indicated microbial communities in Archaean sediments, including evidence for cyanobacteria and eukaryotic precursors.
  • Odd-over-even preference in n-alkanes confirmed syngenetic origin of preserved hydrocarbons.
  • Isotopic offsets between steranes and hopanes suggested metabolic heterogeneity in early ecosystems.
  • Contamination markers were systematically identified and excluded, improving dataset robustness.

Impact

This project illustrates how the integration of biomarker distributions with isotopic measurements can reconstruct the ecological and metabolic structure of ancient environments. By combining contamination-screening protocols with isotope geochemistry, the ANU study provides a robust framework for investigating the origins of microbial ecosystems in the Precambrian.
